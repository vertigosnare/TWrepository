The building is a combination of two styles: an inter-war stripped classical form with extensive Australian Art Deco detailing and decorative elements. The building is faced in sandstone and has a symmetrical U-shaped layout enclosing a four-sided courtyard.

What makes the building so eye-catching (aside from its superb condition) are the strong and consistent use of motifs to reflect the building’s original use as the Australian Institute of Anatomy.

1923

Dr (later Sir) Colin MacKenzie, a distinguished medical scientist, believed that Australia’s unique fauna was destined for extinction. So, he offered to donate his large specimen collection to the Commonwealth, if in return, they established a national anatomical museum.

1924

A site for a national museum of zoology was formally gazette—as envisioned in Walter Burley Griffin’s blueprint for Canberra—with architectural plans including zoological gardens.

1927

A proposal for the National Museum of Australian Zoology was approved, with the building to be located at Acton.

1928–30

The building was designed by Walter Hayward Morris – a top architect from the Federal Capital Commission (FCC). It was one of the last FCC projects finished before the Commission was disbanded in 1930 during the Great Depression.

1931

The National Museum of Australian Zoology changed its name to the Australian Institute of Anatomy to coincide with the opening of the Acton site. Among the many items located at the institute was the heart of legendary race horse Phar Lap.

1984

After being part of the National Library of Australia for 50 years, the NFSA was created as a separate Commonwealth institution. The vacant building became the NFSA’s national headquarters and was opened by Prime Minister Bob Hawke AC. The Australian Institute of Anatomy was abolished.

1991

The remodelled courtyard gardens were officially opened by Prime Minister Bob Hawke’s then wife, Hazel Hawke.

1999

A large annex was added to support the NFSA’s growth to house specialist film, video and audio technical laboratories and curatorial and administrative staff.

2004

The building was added to the Commonwealth Heritage List, comprising natural and cultural heritage places owned or controlled by the Australian Government. The NFSA has a heritage management plan for the long-term protection and conservation of the site.

Today

The building, although modified slightly, still retain the substantially intact original fabric and architectural detail.

The building

The building is a combination of two styles: an inter-war stripped classical form with extensive Australian Art Deco detailing and decorative elements. The building is faced in sandstone and has a symmetrical U-shaped layout enclosing a four-sided courtyard.

What makes the building so eye-catching (aside from its superb condition) are the strong and consistent use of motifs to reflect the building’s original use as the Australian Institute of Anatomy.

Inter-war Art Deco architecture

The emergence of the modern movement was the most significant architectural development during the years between the two World Wars. However, it was a time of intellectual and aesthetic tension between Tradition and Modernism, between people nostalgic for the visual symbols of a pre-industrial past and those who favoured the clean but stark lines and new technologies that characterised Modernism.

The inter-war Art Deco style celebrated the exciting and dynamic aspects of the Machine Age, in an inclusive style that embraced new materials and incorporated graphic decorative elements and modern, eye-catching materials from the past. This approach appealed to more people on an emotional level. So, while not strictly a style of Modernism, there are similarities in influence.

What is Art Deco?

The term comes from a French exhibition in 1925 of ‘Arts Decoratifs’ but did not enter general use until the 1960s to describe a range of inter-war styles. As a term, it captures the spirit of an age when people wanted to put the tragedies and hardships of World War I behind them, to embrace a glamorous, adventurous and elegant future. Art Deco encapsulates changes in style, aesthetics and technology that influenced every visual medium in the 1920s and 1930s.

The style is characterised by a marriage between form and function; by symmetery and balance; by simple, clean lines in buildings, transport, household items and industrial design; and by distinctive decorative embellishments.

Art Deco celebrated the benefits of the Machine Age: industrialisation, mass production, new technologies, quality materials and vibrant colours. It was an era of sky scrapers, ocean liners, railways, motor cars, electricity, bright lights, film, radio and mass entertainment. It marked the beginnings of consumerism.

Key features of Art Deco

  • Symmetry and balance
  • Both vertical and horizontal elements
  • Stepped (also known as ziggurat) forms, both externally and internally
  • Curved or 'streamlined' brickwork and 'eyebrows'
  • New industrial techniques and building materials
  • Decorative motifs symbolic of a building's function
  • Nationalistic Australian Art Deco motifs
  • The principles that decorative elements should be grouped in threes ('the rule of three')
  • Geometric patterning including chevrons, octagons and hexagons

Feature 1 found!

Frilled neck lizards in boomerangs

Carved stone open-mouthed frilled neck lizards framed in stylised boomerangs surround the geometrically patterned front door.

Got a good photo of it?

Use #NFSAfront when you share it on Instagram to get your photo featured below.

Bonus information

If you look around the front door, blue, yellow and green stylised designs inspired by Indigenous Australian bark paintings are displayed in the tiled panels between the windows. Goannas, ferns and waratahs have been carved into the capitals of the columns of the curved portico at the building’s entrance.

Feature 2 found!

Suspended light fittings

Geometric Art Deco patterns are featured throughout the building, in suspended light fittings in the gallery and in the heating and air vents.

Got a good photo of it?

Use #NFSAgeometric when you share it on Instagram to get your photo featured below.

Bonus information

The Southern Gallery, now housing exhibition material from the NFSA’s national audiovisual collection, was originally used to display skeletons, casts of prehistoric skulls, anatomical specimens and models.

Feature 3 found!

Geometrically patterned marble floor

The geometrically patterned marble floor is inspired by Indigenous Australian art. Black marble in the floor and half-columns was quarried from approximately 300 metres away at Acton Flats, now beneath Lake Burley Griffin. This is the only building in Canberra to have this type of marble, as the quarry was virtually exhausted by the end of the project.

Got a good photo of it?

Use #NFSAquarry when you share it on Instagram to get your photo featured below.

Bonus information

The creation of the Australian Institute of Anatomy also involved the establishment of its own unique typeface. Examples can be seen in the front building signage and on the exit signs throughout the building.

Feature 4 found!

Stained glass platypus skylight

The large stained-glass skylight featuring a stylised platypus is a rare example of glazing from the British company Luxfer.

Got a good photo of it?

Use #NFSAluxfer when you share it on Instagram to get your photo featured below.

Bonus information

The platypus features symbolically throughout the building as Sir Colin MacKenzie believed the ancient monotreme was the link between mammal, bird and reptile.

Feature 5 found!

Sir Colin MacKenzie plaque

Along the wall are face masks of famous scientists, including two made from death masks. The Sir Colin MacKenzie commemorative plaque has his ashes interred behind it.

Got a good photo of it?

Use #NFSAcolin when you share it on Instagram to get your photo featured below.

Bonus information

The exterior of the building is faced in Hawkesbury sandstone quarried near Gosford, NSW. The red granite at the base of the exterior and front steps is from Tarana (near Bathurst), NSW and provides a warm contrast.

Feature 6 found!

Bronze cast sculptures of platypus, kookaburra, tree kangaroo, koala and wombat

The walls feature decorative capitals on pilasters with plaster castings of the platypus, kookaburra, tree kangaroo, koala and wombat, painted in bronze to resemble bronze cast sculptures.

Got a good photo of it?

Use #NFSAbronze when you share it on Instagram to get your photo featured below.

Bonus information

The Northern Gallery is now home to the Arc Cinema, which was developed with full sympathy for the building’s unique design and historical significance. Originally it was devoted to displays of applied anatomy, mainly of Australian fauna specimens.

Feature 7 found!

Theatrette

The Theatrette colour scheme is characteristic of early twentieth century theatre halls and is accented by the rich jarrah hardwood floors. The wooden stage includes the original blackboard and screen. The Art Deco patterns are continued in the light fittings and door and wall panelling.

Got a good photo of it?

Use #NFSAred when you share it on Instagram to get your photo featured below.

Bonus information

Newly refurbished, the Theatrette now highlights the original lecture theatre features and can seat up to 114 people.

Did you know there are ghost sightings reported in the building? – stemming from the period it was the Australian Institute of Anatomy. The NFSA Ghost Tour starts in the Theatrette.

Feature 8 found!

Memorial fishpond

The memorial fishpond was dedicated to Sir Colin MacKenzie and was a gift from Lady MacKenzie. It features an ornamental pool with a bronze platypus sculpture and plaque.

Got a good photo of it?

Use #NFSAlady when you share it on Instagram to get your photo featured below.

Bonus information

In 1988, noted Canberra garden designer Cedric Bryant re-landscaped the courtyard, keeping with 1920s Art Deco garden traditions. This garden was opened by Hazel Hawke, the then wife of former Prime Minister Bob Hawke, in September 1991.

Feature 9 found!

Wombat heads within plaques

Wombat heads within plaques are located by the arches that surround the courtyard and continue the symmetry of the building.

Got a good photo of it?

Use #NFSAarch when you share it on Instagram to get your photo featured below.

Bonus information

The fragrant garden, near the café, includes daphne and various coloured lavenders planted under a spreading loquat tree, a species originating from China and Japan which bears abundant fruit each season.

The corner diagonally opposite is predominantly shaded and features winter/spring flowers including camellias, azaleas and pieris.

Back to the Blast

(1981 / PG / 50mins / D: Harry Bardwell)

The screening of this landmark documentary in 1981 triggered a Royal Commission in Australia and House of Lords enquiry in the UK. It covers the mining and shipping of uranium from regional Australia and its eventual use in the British nuclear tests in the South Australian desert.

Daily screenings: 9:15am, 12:00pm, 2:45pm

Silent Storm

(2002 / PG / 55mins / D: Peter Butt)

While the Cold War waged overseas, noted scientist Hedley Marston discovered that our cities and grazing land were being contaminated by radioactive fallout from the British atomic tests in Australia. Marston’s findings were not only disputed, he was targeted as ‘a scientist of counter-espionage interest’.

Daily screenings: 10:40am, 1:30pm, 4:00pm

Ghost Tour

The building is regarded by many ghost hunters or paranormal aficionados as not only the most haunted building in Canberra, but the most haunted in Australia.

Join Tim the Yowie Man for a rare look behind the spooky history of the previous Australian Institute of Anatomy building. Discover the strange, the ghostly and the disturbing stories lurking in the basement and under the stairs – if you dare!

About the NFSA

The National Film and Sound Archive is Australia’s living archive – the custodian of over 2.3 million works that we not only collect, but preserve for future generations and share in many diverse ways. Works include:

  • Films
  • Television
  • Radio programs
  • Videos
  • Audio tapes
  • Records
  • Compact discs
  • Phonographic cylinders, and
  • Wire recordings

It also encompasses documents and artefacts such as:

  • Photographs
  • Posters
  • Lobby cards
  • Publicity items
  • Scripts
  • Costumes
  • Props
  • Memorabilia
  • Oral histories, and
  • Vintage equipment

Getting to the NFSA

We are situated in the heart of Canberra on McCoy Circuit, Acton, between the shores of Lake Burley Griffin and the grounds of the Australian National University. Just a 10-minute walk from the city and state bus interchanges.

Parking

There is dedicated visitor paid parking on the premises between McCoy Circuit and Ellery Cresent. There is also limited free parking surrounding the building on Liversidge Street.
