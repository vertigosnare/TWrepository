The building is a combination of two styles: an inter-war stripped classical form with extensive Australian Art Deco detailing and decorative elements. The building is faced in sandstone and has a symmetrical U-shaped layout enclosing a four-sided courtyard.

What makes the building so eye-catching (aside from its superb condition) are the strong and consistent use of motifs to reflect the building’s original use as the Australian Institute of Anatomy.

1923

Dr (later Sir) Colin MacKenzie, a distinguished medical scientist, believed that Australia’s unique fauna was destined for extinction. So, he offered to donate his large specimen collection to the Commonwealth, if in return, they established a national anatomical museum.

1924

A site for a national museum of zoology was formally gazette—as envisioned in Walter Burley Griffin’s blueprint for Canberra—with architectural plans including zoological gardens.

1927

A proposal for the National Museum of Australian Zoology was approved, with the building to be located at Acton.

1928–30

The building was designed by Walter Hayward Morris – a top architect from the Federal Capital Commission (FCC). It was one of the last FCC projects finished before the Commission was disbanded in 1930 during the Great Depression.

1931

The National Museum of Australian Zoology changed its name to the Australian Institute of Anatomy to coincide with the opening of the Acton site. Among the many items located at the institute was the heart of legendary race horse Phar Lap.

1984

After being part of the National Library of Australia for 50 years, the NFSA was created as a separate Commonwealth institution. The vacant building became the NFSA’s national headquarters and was opened by Prime Minister Bob Hawke AC. The Australian Institute of Anatomy was abolished.

1991

The remodelled courtyard gardens were officially opened by Prime Minister Bob Hawke’s then wife, Hazel Hawke.

1999

A large annex was added to support the NFSA’s growth to house specialist film, video and audio technical laboratories and curatorial and administrative staff.

2004

The building was added to the Commonwealth Heritage List, comprising natural and cultural heritage places owned or controlled by the Australian Government. The NFSA has a heritage management plan for the long-term protection and conservation of the site.

Today

The building, although modified slightly, still retain the substantially intact original fabric and architectural detail.

The building

Inter-war Art Deco architecture

The emergence of the modern movement was the most significant architectural development during the years between the two World Wars. However, it was a time of intellectual and aesthetic tension between Tradition and Modernism, between people nostalgic for the visual symbols of a pre-industrial past and those who favoured the clean but stark lines and new technologies that characterised Modernism.

The inter-war Art Deco style celebrated the exciting and dynamic aspects of the Machine Age, in an inclusive style that embraced new materials and incorporated graphic decorative elements and modern, eye-catching materials from the past. This approach appealed to more people on an emotional level. So, while not strictly a style of Modernism, there are similarities in influence.

What is Art Deco?

The term comes from a French exhibition in 1925 of ‘Arts Decoratifs’ but did not enter general use until the 1960s to describe a range of inter-war styles. As a term, it captures the spirit of an age when people wanted to put the tragedies and hardships of World War I behind them, to embrace a glamorous, adventurous and elegant future. Art Deco encapsulates changes in style, aesthetics and technology that influenced every visual medium in the 1920s and 1930s.

The style is characterised by a marriage between form and function; by symmetery and balance; by simple, clean lines in buildings, transport, household items and industrial design; and by distinctive decorative embellishments.

Art Deco celebrated the benefits of the Machine Age: industrialisation, mass production, new technologies, quality materials and vibrant colours. It was an era of sky scrapers, ocean liners, railways, motor cars, electricity, bright lights, film, radio and mass entertainment. It marked the beginnings of consumerism.

Key features of Art Deco

  • Symmetry and balance
  • Both vertical and horizontal elements
  • Stepped (also known as ziggurat) forms, both externally and internally
  • Curved or 'streamlined' brickwork and 'eyebrows'
  • New industrial techniques and building materials
  • Decorative motifs symbolic of a building's function
  • Nationalistic Australian Art Deco motifs
  • The principles that decorative elements should be grouped in threes ('the rule of three')
  • Geometric patterning including chevrons, octagons and hexagons

Back to the Blast

(1981 / PG / 50mins / D: Harry Bardwell)

The screening of this landmark documentary in 1981 triggered a Royal Commission in Australia and House of Lords enquiry in the UK. It covers the mining and shipping of uranium from regional Australia and its eventual use in the British nuclear tests in the South Australian desert.

Daily screenings: 9:15am, 12:00pm, 2:45pm

Silent Storm

(2002 / PG / 55mins / D: Peter Butt)

While the Cold War waged overseas, noted scientist Hedley Marston discovered that our cities and grazing land were being contaminated by radioactive fallout from the British atomic tests in Australia. Marston’s findings were not only disputed, he was targeted as ‘a scientist of counter-espionage interest’.

Daily screenings: 10:40am, 1:30pm, 4:00pm
